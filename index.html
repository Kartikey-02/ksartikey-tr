Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj

19 February 1630 – 3 April 1680

The King Of Hindu Saamraajy

Shivaji I Shivaji Shahaji Bhonsale; Marathi pronunciation: 19 February 1630 – 3 April 1680 was an Indian ruler and a member of the Bhonsle Maratha clan. Shivaji carved out his own independent kingdom from the declining Adilshahi Sultanate of Bijapur that formed the genesis of the Maratha Empire. In 1674, he was formally crowned the Chhatrapati of his realm at Raigad Fort. Over the course of his life, Shivaji engaged in both alliances and hostilities with the Mughal Empire, the Sultanate of Golkonda, the Sultanate of Bijapur and the European colonial powers. Shivaji's military forces expanded the Maratha sphere of influence, capturing and building forts, and forming a Maratha navy. Shivaji established a competent and progressive civil administration with well-structured administrative institutions. He revived ancient Hindu political traditions, court conventions and promoted the use of the Marathi and Sanskrit languages, replacing Persian at court and in administration. Praised for his chivalrous treatment of women, Shivaji employed people of all castes and religions, including Muslims and Europeans, in his administration and armed forces.

Biographies

  • Born in 1630 in Shivneri Fort, Maharashtra, to Shahaji Bhosale, a prominent Maratha general, and Jijabai, a devout Hindu.
  • Descendant of the Bhonsle Maratha clan, which held influence in the Deccan region.
  • Raised under the guidance of his mother Jijabai, who instilled in him the values of courage, righteousness, and Hindu pride.
  • Received military training from a young age, learning tactics and warfare strategies.
  • Seized the Torna Fort in 1646, marking his first military success and the beginning of his quest for an independent kingdom.
  • Established a small army and gradually expanded his control over several forts and territories, challenging the oppressive rule of the Adil Shahi and Mughal empires.
  • Conducted guerrilla warfare tactics against larger and better-equipped forces, employing hit-and-run strategies and surprise attacks.
  • Notable victories include the capture of Raigad Fort in 1656 and the defeat of the Adil Shahi forces at the Battle of Pratapgad in 1659.

Rani Lakshmibai Biography

19 November 1828 — 18 June 1858

The Warrior Queen of Jhansi

Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi, also known as the "Queen of Jhansi," was a remarkable figure in Indian history during the 19th century. Born in 1828, she emerged as a symbol of resistance against British colonial rule in India during the Indian Rebellion of 1857. Widowed at a young age, Rani Lakshmibai became the ruler of the princely state of Jhansi. She defied societal norms by taking an active role in governance and military affairs. When the Indian Rebellion erupted against British rule, Rani Lakshmibai played a pivotal role in leading her people in the struggle for independence. She fearlessly led her troops into battle, displaying exceptional courage and tactical prowess. Her valiant efforts and determination inspired countless others to join the fight against British oppression.Despite facing overwhelming odds, Rani Lakshmibai's indomitable spirit and fierce determination earned her widespread admiration and respect. However, her resistance ultimately ended in tragedy as she died in battle in 1858. Nevertheless, her legacy as a fearless leader and symbol of Indian nationalism endures, continuing to inspire generations of people in the fight for freedom and justice.

Biographies

  • Born as Manikarnika Tambe, later named Lakshmibai, in Varanasi in 1828.
  • Married Raja Gangadhar Rao of Jhansi at the age of 14.
  • Became the Rani (Queen) of Jhansi when her husband died in 1853, leaving behind an adopted son named Damodar Rao.
  • The British East India Company refused to recognize Damodar Rao as the heir, as he was adopted and not the biological son.
  • During the Indian Rebellion of 1857, Rani Lakshmibai played a significant role in leading the rebellion against British rule.
  • She refused to surrender Jhansi to the British and instead actively participated in military strategy and leadership.
  • Led her forces in the Battle of Jhansi in 1858 against the British.
  • Displayed remarkable courage and military prowess during the battle, fighting on horseback and leading her troops.

Subhas Chandra Bose

23 January 1897 – 18 August 1945

The Revolutionary Visionary

23 January 1897 – 18 August 1945 was an Indian nationalist whose defiance of British authority in India made him a hero among many Indians, but his wartime alliances with Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan left a legacy vexed by authoritarianism, anti-Semitism, and military failure. The honorific 'Netaji' (Bengali: "Respected Leader") was first applied to Bose in Germany in early 1942—by the Indian soldiers of the Indische Legion and by the German and Indian officials in the Special Bureau for India in Berlin. It is now used throughout India. Bose was born into wealth and privilege in a large Bengali family in Orissa during the British Raj. The early recipient of an Anglo-centric education, after college he was sent to England to take the Indian Civil Service examination. He succeeded with distinction in the first exam but demurred at taking the routine final exam, citing nationalism as a higher calling. Returning to India in 1921, Bose joined the nationalist movement led by Mahatma Gandhi and the Indian National Congress. He followed Jawaharlal Nehru to leadership in a group within the Congress which was less keen on constitutional reform and more open to socialism.

Biographies

  • Subhas Chandra Bose was born on January 23, 1897, in Cuttack, Orissa, India, to a prominent Bengali family.
  • He excelled in academics and later studied at Presidency College in Calcutta (now Kolkata) and then went to England to prepare for the Indian Civil Services exams.
  • Bose was deeply influenced by the nationalist movement and the teachings of Swami Vivekananda.
  • He became involved in the Indian National Congress and rose quickly through its ranks, advocating for complete independence from British rule.
  • Bose served as the President of the Indian National Congress in 1938 and 1939, advocating for more aggressive tactics to achieve independence.
  • However, he resigned from the Congress presidency due to ideological differences with Mahatma Gandhi and other leaders regarding the approach to independence.
  • He sought support from various quarters, including Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan, during World War II.
  • He famously declared, "Give me blood, and I shall give you freedom," rallying Indians against British rule.

Bhagat Singh

28 September 1907 – 23 March 1931

The Revolutionary Icon of India's Freedom Struggle

Bhagat Singh 28 September 1907 - 23 March 1931 was an Indian anti-colonial revolutionary, who participated in the mistaken murder of a junior British police officer in December 1928 in what was to be retaliation for the death of an Indian nationalist. He later took part in a largely symbolic bombing of the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi and a hunger strike in jail, which—on the back of sympathetic coverage in Indian-owned newspapers—turned him into a household name in the Punjab region, and after his execution at age 23 into a martyr and folk hero in Northern India. Borrowing ideas from Bolshevism and anarchism, the charismatic Singh electrified a growing militancy in India in the 1930s, and prompted urgent introspection within the Indian National Congress's nonviolent but eventually successful campaign for India's independence. In December 1928, Bhagat Singh and an associate, Shivaram Rajguru, both members of a small revolutionary group, the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (also Army, or HSRA), shot dead a 21-year-old British police officer, John Saunders, in Lahore, Punjab, in what is today Pakistan, mistaking Saunders, who was still on probation, for the British senior police superintendent, James Scott, whom they had intended to assassinate. They held Scott responsible for the death of a popular Indian nationalist leader Lala Lajpat Rai for having ordered a lathi (baton) charge in which Rai was injured and two weeks thereafter died of a heart attack.

Biographies

  • Born on September 28, 1907, in Banga, Punjab, British India.
  • Came from a family deeply involved in the freedom struggle; father Kishan Singh and uncle Ajit Singh were freedom fighters.
  • Influenced by the Jallianwala Bagh massacre (1919) and the non-cooperation movement led by Mahatma Gandhi.
  • oined the Hindustan Republican Association (HRA) at a young age.
  • Participated in the Kakori train robbery (1925) to fund revolutionary activities.
  • MEvolved from Gandhian principles of non-violence to embrace revolutionary ideas of armed struggle.
  • Advocated for complete independence from British rule, rejecting the idea of Dominion status..
  • Courtroom statements during the trial gained him widespread admiration and support among Indians.

The Tribute Website | Chandra Shekhar Sitaram Tiwari

Chandra Shekhar Sitaram Tiwari

23 July 1906 – 27 February 1931

The Revolutionary Icon

Chandra Shekhar Sitaram Tiwari pronunciation 23 July 1906 – 27 February 1931, popularly known as Chandra Shekhar Azad, was an Indian revolutionary who reorganised the Hindustan Republican Association (HRA) under its new name of Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) after the death of its founder, Ram Prasad Bismil, and three other prominent party leaders, Roshan Singh, Rajendra Nath Lahiri and Ashfaqulla Khan. He hailed from Bardarka village in Unnao district of United Provinces and his parents were Sitaram Tiwari and Jagrani Devi. He often used the pseudonym "Balraj" while signing pamphlets issued as the commander-in-chief of the HSRA. Chandra Shekhar Azad was born on 23 July 1906 in Bhabhra village as Chandra Shekhar Tiwari, in a Brahmin family, in the princely-state of Alirajpur. His forefathers were from Badarka village of Unnao district of Uttar Pradesh. His mother, Jagrani Devi, was the third wife of Sitaram Tiwari, whose previous wives had died young. After the birth of their first son, Sukhdev, in Badarka, the family moved to Alirajpur State. His mother wanted her son to be a great Sanskrit scholar and persuaded his father to send him to Kashi Vidyapeeth at Banaras to study. In 1921, when the Non-Cooperation Movement was at its height, Chandra Shekhar, then a 15-year-old student, joined. As a result, he was arrested on 20 December. On being presented before the Parsi district magistrate Justice M. P. Khareghat a week later, he gave his name as "Azad" (The Free), his father's name as "Swatantrata" (Independence) and his residence as "Jail". The angered magistrate punished him with 15 lashes.

Biographies

  • Born on July 23, 1906, in Bhavra village, present-day Alirajpur district, Madhya Pradesh, India.
  • From a young age, Azad was deeply influenced by the nationalist movement and the ideas of freedom fighters like Ram Prasad Bismil and Bhagat Singh.
  • Joined the Non-Cooperation Movement at the age of 15, inspired by Mahatma Gandhi's call for nonviolent resistance against British rule.
  • Azad played a significant role in the Kakori Conspiracy of 1925, a train robbery aimed at funding revolutionary activities against British rule.
  • Collaborated closely with Bhagat Singh and other prominent revolutionaries, sharing the vision of a socialist and egalitarian India.
  • Fought valiantly against British police in the famous Battle of Alfred Park (now Azad Park) in Allahabad on February 27, 1931, where he preferred death over surrender.
  • Chandra Shekhar Azad's martyrdom left an indelible mark on India's struggle for independence, inspiring future generations to continue the fight against colonial oppression.
  • Azad is revered as a national hero in India, with numerous memorials.

A . P . J Abdul Kalam

1931 - 2015

Missile Man of India

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam 15 October 1931 – 27 July 2015 was an Indian aerospace scientist who served as the 11th president of India from 2002 to 2007. He was born and raised in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu and studied physics and aerospace engineering. He spent the next four decades as a scientist and science administrator, mainly at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and was intimately involved in India's civilian space programme and military missile development efforts.[1] He thus came to be known as the Missile Man of India for his work on the development of ballistic missile and launch vehicle technology.[2][3][4] He also played a pivotal organisational, technical, and political role in India's Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, the first since the original nuclear test by India in 1974.
